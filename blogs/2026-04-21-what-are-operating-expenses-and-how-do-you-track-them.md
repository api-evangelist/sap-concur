<h2>What are operating expenses?&nbsp;</h2>

<p>Operating expenses (shortened to OpEx) are the ongoing costs to run a business. Think about recurring expenses needed to maintain business operations and earn revenue. These aren't capital expenditures which involve buying long-term assets, but expenses consumed within the current accounting period. Operating expenses are things such as rent, salaries, utilities and supplies, whereas capital expenditures (CapEx) includes things like buildings and equipment that depreciate over time and have long term value.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Operating expenses have a major influence on cash flow and profitability, as they are baseline costs for running a business and often the largest portion of total expenses. They can often give you deep insight into how efficient and sustainable your business model is, and companies that have this understanding generally are more financially stable.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Operating expenses can be divided into <a href="https://www.concur.com/blog/article/mastering-budget-categories-for-better-financial-management">fixed or variable categories</a>. Fixed expenses such as rent or insurance remain constant, while variable expenses (such as sales commissions) can fluctuate over time. This distinction is important for understanding cost control and budgeting, as fixed expenses can become an issue during slow periods and variable expenses must be monitored over time as business needs change.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Some other categories of operational expenses include the following:&nbsp;</p>

<p>Infrastructure costs or facility costs&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Personnel costs&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Marketing or client acquisition costs&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Office supplies and materials&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Travel and entertainment&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Professional services&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Insurance and risk management&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>Another distinction is <a href="https://www.concur.com/blog/article/direct-vs-indirect-spend">direct vs. indirect spend</a>, with direct spend being expenses tied to producing goods and services and indirect spend being expenses that are not directly tied. Think raw materials (direct) vs. rent (indirect).&nbsp;</p>

<h2>Why is tracking operating expenses important?&nbsp;</h2>

<p>Now that we've talked about expenses, let's walk through why expense visibility is so important.&nbsp;</p>

<p>To run your business effectively, you need financial transparency and visibility. Opacity is the enemy of efficient management, as leaders can only optimize and spot financial problem areas when they know precisely how money is being spent.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Visibility into operating expenses is crucial because it lets you make data-informed decisions about choosing or retaining vendors, resource allocation, and what to prioritize in terms of spending. If you can see which departments or projects are consuming the most resources and measure that against performance, you can begin to optimize your resource deployment.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Getting a handle on your expense patterns also enables you to benchmark vs. industry standards, internal numbers and trends over time. Running this kind of analysis can show if spending levels are justified and shows potential improvement areas.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Visibility into expense data also enables accurate budget planning and forecasting, as it becomes possible to identify cost patterns and seasonal variations, along with expenses related to growth. Sharper, more feasible budgets help better prepare you for the financial needs of the future.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Finding financial risks before they spiral out of control should be a priority for all financial leaders, and expense visibility can help pinpoint odd spending patterns, accelerated cost increases, and vendor/client concentration risk.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Finally, if you have detailed expense analysis, it becomes easier to spot inefficiencies and consolidation opportunities. All those duplicate software subscriptions, and underused services add up.&nbsp;</p>

<h2>How to manage operating expenses more effectively&nbsp;</h2>

<p>Before we conclude, let's run through some guidance for effective expense management, showing you how to improve your capabilities while minimizing unnecessary costs.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Regular expense reviews can help you spot trends and unusual spending patterns and give you the insight you need for optimization. Make sure to get all department managers onboard so you have a holistic view.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Negotiate your vendor contracts in a strategic way to ensure good service and fair pricing. If you can consolidate to leverage volume pricing, consider doing so, but don't assume the risk that comes with vendor over-consolidation.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Compare your actual expenses with your budgeted amounts to understand any variance. This will help you spot any area where spending is out of control and take fast corrective steps.&nbsp;</p>
