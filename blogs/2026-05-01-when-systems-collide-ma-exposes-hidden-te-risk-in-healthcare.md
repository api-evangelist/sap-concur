<p>Healthcare M&amp;A moves fast. Integration&nbsp;doesn’t.&nbsp;But&nbsp;organizations&nbsp;with regulatory-grade T&amp;E platforms can&nbsp;establish&nbsp;spend&nbsp;visibility in weeks, not months.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p>This is critical when CMS&nbsp;requires&nbsp;accurate&nbsp;cost reporting within&nbsp;150 days&nbsp;of close&nbsp;after the effective date of the provider agreement termination or the change of ownership. Others spend&nbsp;6 to&nbsp;18 months&nbsp;reconciling duplicate payments, conflicting policies, and fragmented data, risking Medicare/Medicaid reimbursement delays and audit penalties.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Most system integration plans are designed around a&nbsp;6 to 12 month&nbsp;timeline. But in healthcare, regulatory compliance&nbsp;can’t&nbsp;wait. While systems combine, the organization must&nbsp;maintain:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Separate cost center reporting&nbsp;for CMS reimbursement&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Jurisdiction-specific travel policies&nbsp;across multi-state operations&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Clinician credentialing documentation&nbsp;tied to expense records&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Audit trails&nbsp;that satisfy Joint Commission and state health department requirements&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>Integration&nbsp;doesn't&nbsp;follow the plan because compliance&nbsp;doesn't&nbsp;pause for&nbsp;M&amp;A.&nbsp;</p>

<h2>The Promise Is Big. So Is the Pressure.&nbsp;</h2>

<p>Every merger starts with a vision for:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Better access:&nbsp;Expanded service areas and specialist coverage&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Stronger outcomes:&nbsp;Standardized care protocols across facilities&nbsp;</li>
	<li>More time:&nbsp;Caregivers focus on patients instead of processes.&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>At&nbsp;scale, those outcomes matter, especially when millions of patients and tens of thousands of employees are involved.&nbsp;&nbsp;</p>

<p>But&nbsp;delivering&nbsp;that vision depends on something less visible:&nbsp;Can you&nbsp;maintain&nbsp;regulatory compliance and financial control while&nbsp;operating&nbsp;across legacy entities?&nbsp;</p>

<h2>What Healthcare M&amp;A Integration Actually Requires&nbsp;</h2>

<ol>
	<li><strong>Multi-Entity Cost Reporting&nbsp;</strong><br />
	Multi-entity&nbsp;cost&nbsp;reporting&nbsp;requires&nbsp;accurate&nbsp;cost allocation at the legal entity (provider) level. During M&amp;A,&nbsp;organizations&nbsp;must&nbsp;maintain&nbsp;separate&nbsp;reporting&nbsp;structures until full legal and financial consolidation is complete.&nbsp;<br />
	&nbsp;</li>
	<li><strong>Clinician-Specific Compliance&nbsp;</strong><br />
	Clinician-related spend must be documented in ways that support compliance with federal transparency and fraud-and-abuse regulations, while also enabling&nbsp;accurate&nbsp;cost attribution across facilities for audit and reimbursement purposes.&nbsp;<br />
	&nbsp;</li>
	<li><strong>Multi-Jurisdiction Policy Management</strong><br />
	Healthcare organizations must manage varying policy requirements across states, labor agreements, and facility types, each of which can influence allowable expenses,&nbsp;reimbursement of&nbsp;treatment, and audit expectations.&nbsp;<br />
	&nbsp;</li>
	<li><strong>Audit-Ready Documentation&nbsp;</strong><br />
	Audit and accreditation bodies,&nbsp;including Medicare auditors and state regulators,&nbsp;require&nbsp;defensible documentation of financial and operational activities. While not always reviewing T&amp;E directly, inadequate documentation can expose gaps in cost allocation, compliance, and internal controls.&nbsp;</li>
</ol>

<p><strong>The Bottom line:</strong>&nbsp;Generic spend platforms treat M&amp;A as a consolidation exercise. In healthcare, organizations must&nbsp;maintain&nbsp;audit-ready, entity-aware&nbsp;spend&nbsp;controls throughout integration. This ensures compliance,&nbsp;accurate&nbsp;cost allocation, and defensible reporting across legacy structures.&nbsp;</p>

<h2>The&nbsp;Moment&nbsp;You Feel&nbsp;It:&nbsp;Fragmentation Overnight&nbsp;</h2>

<p>Before the merger, things feel manageable. Teams know their systems. Policies are understood, even if they&nbsp;aren’t&nbsp;perfect. Workflows move, even with friction.&nbsp;Then an M&amp;A integration begins, and suddenly:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Expense policies&nbsp;don’t&nbsp;align&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Corporate card programs&nbsp;don’t&nbsp;reconcile cleanly&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Approval structures overlap or conflict&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Travel and expense systems&nbsp;operate&nbsp;side by side, but not together&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>What once felt controlled now feels unclear. Finance teams&nbsp;start&nbsp;asking:&nbsp;<em>Which number is right?</em>&nbsp;IT teams start emailing:&nbsp;<em>Where is this data coming from?&nbsp;</em>Employees start&nbsp;to panic:&nbsp;<em>What&nbsp;policy&nbsp;am I supposed to follow?</em></p>

<p>The&nbsp;transition&nbsp;isn’t&nbsp;technical at&nbsp;first.&nbsp;It’s&nbsp;emotional.&nbsp;Your&nbsp;employee's&nbsp;confidence drops before systems are fixed.&nbsp;</p>

<h3>Why M&amp;A Surfaces T&amp;E&nbsp;Risk So Quickly&nbsp;</h3>

<p>Mergers&nbsp;don’t&nbsp;introduce&nbsp;new problems; they simply remove the buffer that was hiding them.&nbsp;When two different healthcare&nbsp;T&amp;E&nbsp;environments combine, small inconsistencies become visible&nbsp;at&nbsp;scale.&nbsp;This can often look like:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Duplicate reimbursements that were once isolated now overlap&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Out-of-policy spend becomes harder to track across entities&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Disconnected data creates multiple versions of the truth&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Manual reconciliation increases as systems&nbsp;fail to&nbsp;align&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>At this level of scale, even small inconsistencies&nbsp;don’t&nbsp;stay small. They repeat, multiply, and spread across teams, regions, and systems. This turns minor gaps into operational risks for healthcare compliance teams. If&nbsp;you're&nbsp;navigating this transition, this&nbsp;<a href="https://www.concur.com/resource-center/guides/merger-acquisition-travel-expense-integration">M&amp;A travel and expense integration guide</a>&nbsp;outlines where these risks tend to surface first.&nbsp;</p>

<h3>The Risk Window: When Clarity&nbsp;Hasn’t&nbsp;Caught Up Yet&nbsp;&nbsp;</h3>

<p>There’s&nbsp;a phase in every integration that&nbsp;doesn’t&nbsp;get enough attention: The middle.&nbsp;Like&nbsp;the&nbsp;middle child,&nbsp;it’s&nbsp;neither exciting as the beginning nor&nbsp;the&nbsp;end.&nbsp;It’s&nbsp;where systems&nbsp;aren’t&nbsp;fully aligned, policies&nbsp;are still shifting, and teams&nbsp;are&nbsp;operating&nbsp;with partial clarity.&nbsp;And&nbsp;it’s&nbsp;where outcomes&nbsp;are often decided.&nbsp;</p>

<p>A 2023 survey by PwC found that just&nbsp;14% of companies&nbsp;report achieving substantial success across strategic, operational, and financial goals after a merger.&nbsp;In healthcare, that gap is often tied to what happens after the deal closes.&nbsp;Or where integration becomes the challenge, where spend&nbsp;visibility starts to break down, and where compliance risk begins to rise.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Not&nbsp;slowly,&nbsp;but all at once.&nbsp;This is often when&nbsp;organizations start looking for&nbsp;a&nbsp;structure. How to align systems, policies, and data before&nbsp;the&nbsp;gaps widen.&nbsp;A practical starting point is&nbsp;<a href="https://www.concur.com/resource-center/whitepapers/mergers-acquisitions-and-your-sap-concur-solution-best-practices-for-te-leaders">understanding how integration impacts T&amp;E workflows</a>&nbsp;end to end.&nbsp;How to&nbsp;recognize&nbsp;the&nbsp;M&amp;A&nbsp;risk window:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Policies&nbsp;haven’t&nbsp;been unified&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Systems&nbsp;aren’t&nbsp;fully connected&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Employees are doing their best with partial information&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>Areas&nbsp;where risk quietly expands:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Expenses&nbsp;submitted&nbsp;under legacy rules that no longer apply&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Corporate card transactions that&nbsp;don’t&nbsp;match new structures&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Travel booked outside preferred channels&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Approvals that follow outdated hierarchies&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<h3>Why Healthcare Feels This More&nbsp;Deeply&nbsp;</h3>

<p>No one is trying to create risk, but the experience feels uncertain, and uncertainty leads to inconsistency. For finance leaders, this shows up in ways that are hard to ignore:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Slower close cycles&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>More audit exceptions&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Less confidence in reported spend&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>In healthcare, T&amp;E&nbsp;is&nbsp;connected directly to how care is delivered. When systems are aligned, clinicians spend less time navigating processes and more time with patients. When they&nbsp;aren’t, friction shows up in small but meaningful ways, such as&nbsp;delays, confusion, and time pulled away from care.&nbsp;</p>

<p>The impact goes beyond finance, such as:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Compliance tracking becomes inconsistent&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Data moves across systems without clear governance&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Sensitive information becomes harder to control&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>For IT and Security leaders, the pressure builds quickly.&nbsp;Because this&nbsp;isn’t&nbsp;just integration.&nbsp;It’s&nbsp;data security, policy enforcement, and operational trust&nbsp;all at once.&nbsp;</p>

<h2>Healthcare&nbsp;Compliance Doesn't Pause for Integration&nbsp;</h2>

<p>In healthcare, compliance keeps moving even when systems&nbsp;don’t.&nbsp;During M&amp;A, policies, data, and workflows are all in transition at once. And in that movement, the structure that supports compliance starts to loosen in subtle ways:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>An audit trail&nbsp;doesn’t&nbsp;fully carry over&nbsp;</li>
	<li>A provider’s travel history sits across multiple systems&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Sensitive data moves, but not always with the same level of control&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>Nothing is technically “broken.” But&nbsp;it’s&nbsp;no longer fully intact.&nbsp;And that’s where risk lives. Not in obvious gaps, but in partial visibility. In moments where tracking, validation, and oversight require more effort than they should.&nbsp;</p>

<p>Compliance&nbsp;isn’t&nbsp;about having controls.&nbsp;It’s&nbsp;about whether they&nbsp;hold&nbsp;when&nbsp;things&nbsp;move.&nbsp;</p>

<h3>Beyond General Integration&nbsp;</h3>

<p>In healthcare, T&amp;E touches compliance, clinician movement, and daily operations.&nbsp;What looks like standard integration on the surface quickly becomes something more specialized underneath:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li><strong>HIPAA-compliant </strong>expense data handling&nbsp;as systems transition and sensitive data moves between environments&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li><strong>Provider credentialing travel tracking&nbsp;</strong>across newly merged entities, where clinician movement must remain visible and compliant&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li><strong>Multi-state licensure requirements</strong>&nbsp;for traveling clinicians, where policy and reimbursement must align with regulatory boundaries&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li><strong>Joint Commission audit trails</strong>&nbsp;tied to education and CME-related spend, where documentation must remain intact across systems&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li><strong>Stark Law and Anti-Kickback </strong>considerations&nbsp;in vendor and travel relationships, where financial transparency is critical&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li><strong>Revenue cycle implications&nbsp;</strong>where T&amp;E data influences cost-to-serve and operational decision-making&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>This is where many platforms fall short.&nbsp;</p>

<h2>Before the Merge: Creating Stability&nbsp;</h2>

<p>The healthcare organizations that move through integration with confidence&nbsp;don’t&nbsp;wait for issues to surface. They look for where friction will&nbsp;feel&nbsp;highest and address it early.&nbsp;That starts with visibility:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Where do systems overlap or conflict?&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>How different are expense policies across entities?&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Where might data lose structure during integration?&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Which areas of&nbsp;spend&nbsp;are hardest to track today?&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>These questions&nbsp;don’t&nbsp;just&nbsp;identify&nbsp;gaps. They create alignment before complexity increases. A common shared vision.&nbsp;</p>

<h2>After the Merge: A 90-Day Path to Stability&nbsp;</h2>

<p>Once integration begins, speed&nbsp;matters,&nbsp;but clarity matters more. The goal&nbsp;isn’t&nbsp;to fix everything at once.&nbsp;It’s&nbsp;to reduce uncertainty where it matters most.&nbsp;The organizations that regain control fastest tend to follow a phased approach:&nbsp;</p>

<h3><strong>Days 1–30: Stabilize&nbsp;</strong></h3>

<p>In the early days, the priority is reducing confusion and creating a baseline everyone can rely on.&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Establish a&nbsp;unified reporting view, even if backend systems are still separate&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Pause major policy changes and&nbsp;communicate clear interim expectations&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Identify&nbsp;high-risk spend categories&nbsp;(executive travel, clinical conferences, vendor relationships)&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>At this stage, the goal is simple: Create enough structure that teams can&nbsp;operate&nbsp;without second-guessing every decision.&nbsp;</p>

<h3><strong>Days 31–60: Harmonize&nbsp;</strong></h3>

<p>Once the environment stabilizes, alignment becomes the focus.&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Consolidate&nbsp;corporate card programs&nbsp;under consistent terms&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Align&nbsp;approval workflows and hierarchies&nbsp;with the new organizational structure&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Begin&nbsp;migrating historical data&nbsp;to support trend analysis and audit readiness&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>This is where fragmentation starts to&nbsp;resolve,&nbsp;and consistency begins to return.&nbsp;</p>

<h3>Days 61–90:&nbsp;Optimize&nbsp;</h3>

<p>With systems and policies aligned, organizations can shift toward long-term control.&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Launch a&nbsp;unified policy framework&nbsp;across all entities&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Train employees on&nbsp;standardized processes and systems&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Establish an&nbsp;ongoing governance model&nbsp;with automated compliance checks&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>At this point, the organization moves from reacting to integration&nbsp;to&nbsp;operating&nbsp;with confidence.&nbsp;&nbsp;This phased approach creates:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Clarity early, when uncertainty is highest&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Consistency next, as systems and teams align&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Control over time, as governance becomes embedded&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>And in healthcare where compliance, scale, and care delivery are all connected, that progression matters. Because the goal&nbsp;isn’t&nbsp;just to integrate systems.&nbsp;It’s&nbsp;to restore confidence in the data, the process, and decisions that follow.&nbsp;</p>

<p>M&amp;A is often framed as a growth strategy. But in practice,&nbsp;it’s&nbsp;a moment of clarity that reveals how well&nbsp;systems&nbsp;connect,&nbsp;policies translate, and teams can&nbsp;operate&nbsp;with confidence through change.&nbsp;Building confidence in your new process with employees and HCPs comes from knowing that the:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Data is&nbsp;accurate&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Policies are clear&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Systems are aligned&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Outcomes are predictable&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>That’s&nbsp;when&nbsp;finance&nbsp;can move forward. When IT can trust the infrastructure. And when leaders can make decisions without second-guessing the numbers.&nbsp;And in healthcare, where scale, complexity, and delivery&nbsp;of care&nbsp;intersect, that clarity&nbsp;carries weight.&nbsp;That means:&nbsp;</p>

<ul>
	<li>Reviewing and evolving policies regularly&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Paying attention to ambiguous&nbsp;spend&nbsp;categories that signal&nbsp;emerging&nbsp;gaps&nbsp;&nbsp;</li>
	<li>Designing governance frameworks that scale&nbsp;as the organization grows&nbsp;</li>
</ul>

<p>Because the goal&nbsp;isn’t&nbsp;just integration.&nbsp;It’s&nbsp;an environment where control, clarity, and confidence exist together.&nbsp;</p>
